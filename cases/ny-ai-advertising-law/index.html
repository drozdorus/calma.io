New York's AI Advertising Law: What Lead Gen Operators Need to Know

NYC Local Law 144 and upcoming state-level AI transparency rules — what they require, who they affect, and how to stay compliant.

If you run performance lead gen campaigns targeting New York consumers, you're operating in a regulatory environment that has moved fast. New York signed its first standalone AI advertising law in December 2025 — the Synthetic Performer Disclosure Act (SB-8420A), which takes effect June 9, 2026. But that one law is only the most visible layer of a broader picture: a patchwork of federal rules, existing state statutes, and additional legislation in motion already creates real obligations that predate it.

This article covers what applies right now, what recently passed, how neighboring states set the precedent NY followed, and what lead gen operators should be doing today.

What's Already in Force

FTC Section 5: The Floor That Applies Everywhere

The Federal Trade Commission's authority over unfair or deceptive acts and practices (Section 5 of the FTC Act) is the baseline for AI-related advertising compliance across all 50 states, including New York. The FTC has been explicit since 2023 that AI doesn't create an exemption — it creates new ways to violate existing rules.

Two updates matter most for lead gen operators. First, the FTC's revised Endorsement Guides (effective July 26, 2023) explicitly address AI-generated testimonials and reviews. If your landing pages or ads include testimonials produced or enhanced by AI, disclosure is required — "results not typical" language is not sufficient on its own. The guides also clarify that fake reviews (including those generated by AI tools) are a per se violation.

Second, in 2023–2024 the FTC issued policy statements and guidance directly naming AI-driven targeting and personalization as areas of scrutiny. The FTC's position: using AI to exploit consumer vulnerabilities — financial stress, health anxiety, age — in ad targeting is potentially an unfair practice regardless of whether a specific AI law exists. For insurance and loan lead gen, where your audience selection by definition skews toward people in difficult situations, this is not an abstract concern.

NY SHIELD Act

New York's Stop Hacks and Improve Electronic Data Security Act (SHIELD Act, effective March 2020) imposes data security obligations on any business that holds private information of New York residents — regardless of where the business is located. If you're collecting leads from NY consumers, it applies to you.

The SHIELD Act doesn't mention AI, but it has direct relevance: if you're feeding first-party lead data into AI training pipelines, scoring models, or third-party targeting platforms, your data security and reasonable safeguard obligations still attach to that data. A breach or unauthorized use of data used in an AI model doesn't get a pass because it was anonymized upstream.

The practical read: SHIELD Act compliance means knowing exactly where your NY lead data flows after collection — including into any AI enrichment, scoring, or suppression vendors. Document it. That documentation will matter when regulators ask.

New York General Business Law § 349

NY GBL § 349, which prohibits deceptive acts or practices in trade or commerce, is enforced by both the NY Attorney General and private plaintiffs. It has been used against digital advertising practices well before AI was a mainstream topic. AI-generated content that is materially misleading — fabricated quotes, synthetic before/after imagery, fake expert endorsements — falls squarely within its scope. Through early 2026, no major NY AG enforcement actions have specifically cited AI-generated ad content, but the AG's office has been active on adjacent issues: in January 2026, it sent a formal information request to Instacart under New York's Algorithmic Pricing Disclosure Act, signaling growing willingness to use existing and new statutes against AI-driven commercial practices.

NYC and NY State: What's Passed and What's Coming

Local Law 144: Employment, Not Advertising — But Read It Anyway

New York City's Local Law 144, which took effect July 2023, requires employers and employment agencies using Automated Employment Decision Tools (AEDTs) to conduct bias audits and provide candidate notifications. It is scoped entirely to employment decisions — hiring, promotion, compensation.

It does not apply to advertising. It does not regulate how you target ads or generate creatives. We mention it here because it's frequently cited incorrectly in marketing compliance discussions, and because its structure — mandatory audits, disclosure requirements, third-party oversight — is exactly the template that proposed advertising-focused AI legislation is following.

What NY State Enacted — and What's Still in the Pipeline

On December 11, 2025, Governor Hochul signed SB-8420A, amending New York's General Business Law to require conspicuous disclosure whenever advertisements feature a "synthetic performer" — a digitally created human image generated using AI or software algorithms, not recognizable as any real, identifiable person. The law takes effect June 9, 2026, with civil penalties of $1,000 for a first violation and $5,000 for subsequent violations.

This is narrower than it might sound. The disclosure requirement applies specifically to AI-generated human-appearing performers in ads — it does not regulate AI-written copy, AI-targeted audiences, or automated decision-making in lead scoring. For lead gen operators, the immediate practical question is: does any creative in your library feature a generated human face or likeness? If yes, a clear disclosure is required after June 9.

The legislative pipeline still contains broader proposals: algorithmic accountability bills requiring impact assessments for high-risk automated decision systems (relevant to insurance and loan lead scoring), and state-level companion bills to Local Law 144. None of these had been signed as of this writing, but the bipartisan legislative activity continues into the 2026 session.

What Other States Have Done: The Bellwether Picture

NY is a laggard in AI-specific legislation compared to several peers. Understanding what's already in force elsewhere matters because (a) your campaigns likely run nationally, and (b) NY will borrow from these frameworks when it does act.

Colorado SB24-205

Colorado's AI Act (SB24-205), signed May 2024, is the most comprehensive US state AI law to date. It requires developers and deployers of "high-risk" AI systems — defined as consequential decision-making in areas like insurance, lending, employment, and housing — to conduct impact assessments, implement risk management programs, and provide consumer notifications and appeal rights. Insurance is an explicitly covered consequential decision domain, which means AI systems used in underwriting pipelines that your leads feed into fall within scope.

Implementation has been delayed: the original effective date of February 2026 was pushed to June 30, 2026 by SB 25B-004, signed August 2025. Additionally, a Colorado court ordered the AG not to enforce the law pending finalization of implementing rules. The law is real and the compliance framework is set — but active enforcement is on hold while rulemaking is completed. Colorado's framework remains the leading model for states, including New York, considering comprehensive AI regulation.

California

California's CPRA automated decision-making regulations were finalized by the California Privacy Protection Agency in September 2025. Risk assessment requirements took effect January 1, 2026; full ADMT opt-out compliance (consumers' right to opt out of automated decision-making that uses their personal information) takes effect January 1, 2027. If you collect leads from California residents and use AI scoring or targeting models, the compliance clock has already started. California has also passed multiple synthetic media disclosure laws for political advertising, with commercial advertising regulation following in the pipeline.

Illinois BIPA

Illinois' Biometric Information Privacy Act is relevant if any of your creative production or targeting infrastructure touches biometric data — face geometry, voiceprints. AI image generation using real people's faces, AI voice cloning for ad voiceovers — both have BIPA exposure if the underlying data collection involved Illinois residents.

The emerging pattern: NY's SB-8420A followed the same template as Texas (HB 4337), California (AB 602), and others — mandatory disclosure for AI-generated human likenesses in commercial content. The next wave — impact assessments, opt-out rights, private right of action — is already in the Albany pipeline. The structural elements are predictable. Compliance planning can get specific now.

Practical Implications for Lead Gen Operators

AI-Generated Creatives and Testimonials

FTC rules already require disclosure when testimonials are AI-generated or when results are not typical. The standard is whether a consumer would be misled, not whether you technically labeled something. If your landing page features a quote from "Sarah, 54, saved $400/year on home insurance" and that quote was AI-generated, you have a disclosure obligation — and potentially a deception liability — under current law.

AI-generated synthetic performers in ad creatives are now a specific legal risk in New York. SB-8420A, effective June 9, 2026, requires a conspicuous disclosure whenever an advertisement includes a synthetic performer — a generated human likeness not recognizable as any real person. Several other states (Texas, California, Illinois) have analogous laws or proposed legislation. The safe approach for any nationally-running campaign: implement disclosure language now, uniformly, across all markets.

Automated Targeting and Audience Segmentation

AI-driven bidding and audience models are not exempt from consumer protection scrutiny. If your targeting model systematically excludes or includes audiences on the basis of protected characteristics — even indirectly through proxy variables — you have exposure under fair lending laws (ECOA, FHA) and FTC unfairness standards, in addition to emerging AI-specific rules.

Document your targeting methodology. Know what your third-party data providers are putting into models. If you use a demand-side platform that uses AI bidding, get their terms in writing regarding fair lending compliance. Regulators have started asking these questions in investigations.

Consent Language and Lead Scoring

If you sell leads to buyers who use AI scoring to decide whether to contact a consumer, your TCPA consent language and privacy disclosures should reflect the downstream use. "May be processed by automated decision-making systems" is the kind of disclosure that future NY rules will likely require. Adding it now costs almost nothing and creates documented evidence of good-faith compliance practice.

Data Pipelines into AI Models

NY SHIELD Act obligations follow your data. If NY consumer PII flows into an AI training dataset — even a third party's model — your security and oversight obligations apply. Run a data flow audit with specific attention to enrichment vendors, co-registration partners, and any third parties with model training access to your lead data.

Compliance Checklist: What to Do Now

  • Audit AI-generated content. Flag every piece of creative (copy, imagery, video, testimonials) that was AI-generated or AI-enhanced. Apply FTC Endorsement Guide disclosures where required.
  • Map your data flows. Document where NY consumer data goes after collection — enrichment, scoring, suppression, model training. Confirm SHIELD Act safeguards apply at each handoff.
  • Review targeting methodology documentation. Ensure you can explain and defend your audience segmentation logic. For insurance and loan verticals, run a fair lending screen on any AI-driven targeting models.
  • Update consent and privacy language. Add forward-looking disclosure language covering automated decision-making in your lead capture forms and privacy policies. Align with CCPA language as the de facto national standard while NY rules develop.
  • Vet your AI vendors. Get written representations from DSPs, scoring vendors, and creative tools about their compliance posture. Specifically: do they audit for discriminatory outputs? Do they provide documentation?
  • Comply with SB-8420A by June 9, 2026. Audit all ad creatives for AI-generated human likenesses. Any creative using a synthetic performer requires a conspicuous disclosure. Then continue monitoring the NY legislative calendar — the pipeline still contains broader algorithmic accountability bills with shorter implementation windows when they pass.
  • Consider a Colorado SB24-205 readiness assessment. If you run any volume in Colorado with insurance or loan verticals, assess your high-risk AI obligations now. The same assessment will transfer directly to NY when comparable legislation passes.

The Trajectory: What Comes After SB-8420A

New York's SB-8420A is the first law — not the last. The Synthetic Performer Disclosure Act addresses one specific AI use case in advertising: generated human likenesses. The broader regulatory agenda — algorithmic accountability, automated decision-making in financial products, data pipeline oversight — remains active at both the city and state level. Federal action through FTC rulemaking and potential congressional AI legislation layers on top, not in replacement.

The next concrete deadline is June 9, 2026. After that, the legislative pipeline at Albany will produce additional requirements. The timeline is uncertain; the direction is not. The compliance work — creative audits, data flow mapping, disclosure language, vendor vetting — is not preparation for a hypothetical future. It is the actual work of operating in this market now.

AI regulation in advertising has arrived in New York. It started narrow. It will not stay that way.
